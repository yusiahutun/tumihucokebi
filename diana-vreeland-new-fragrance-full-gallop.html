Diana Vreeland's legacy and love of fragrance lives on

September 18, 2024
blog

Diana Vreeland. Image: Getty

Diana Vreeland Parfums is now a 10-strong band of luxury fragrances inspired by the late style icon and former Harper's Bazaar visionary. Created by her grandson Alexander Vreeland, himself a long-standing servant to fashion who has worked in public relations for Ralph Lauren and Giorgio Armani, the latest in the range is inspired by Vreeland's love of dance.

"She never wrote," he explains at the launch of Full Gallop, the tenth perfume in the collection. "She would dictate – the usage of accentuation in the language and the elongation of certain words she always felt came from her passion for dance."

Indeed, it was Vreeland's love of dance that first landed her in the Harper's Bazaar editor Carmel Snow's office back in 1936.

Vreeland, who would go on to work for Bazaar for 26 years before moving to Vogue, was dancing on the roof of New York's St Regis Hotel with her husband one night when she was spotted by Snow, who asked her to come and visit the magazine's office. "She went and within months she was doing the Why Don't You columns, which was really the beginning of her career at Harper's Bazaar," he explains.  

Full Gallop, created by the perfumer Carlos Benaim, has a base of heliotrope and orris, with notes of amber, Moroccan jasmine, coriander, Turkish rose and osmanthus.

Diana Vreeland

The latest Diana Vreeland Parfums launch, Full Gallop

"A lot of our other fragrances are much more bold," explains Vreeland. "What we loved about this one is that it has a certain kind of gentleness and approachableness. We started working on the name and packaging after the juice – if you get lost in the name and the story of a fragrance, the juice gets lost."

The "juice" was of huge importance, too, to Mrs Vreeland, who would douse her belongings in her favourite perfumes.

"Fragrance was embedded in everything she did," explains her grandson. "She always wore it and her homes were always highly fragranced. She had pot pourri and a vase with all these incenses burning, then she would spray the room with fragrance. Finally, she would inject fragrance into pillows with a hypodermic needle – all at the same time!"

Two of her favourites were Chanel No 5 and Yves Saint Laurent's Opium – but Vreeland was careful not to get too "historical" when creating his line of fragrances. "The story should be: if she was 35 today, what would she wear?" he says. "I wanted to be very careful for it not to smell nostalgic. I didn't ever want to say 'We want to re-do Chanel No 5'. I wanted to create beautiful, relevant fragrances."

"Relevant" is another word Vreeland uses to describe his grandmother, who passed away in 1989.

"The continuity [of that] has been fascinating," he says. "The most important thing about her was that everything she touched, she really changed dramatically. The woman she was photographing for Bazaar was an intelligent one – a beautiful one, but one you wanted to meet. She had the courage to use that calibre of woman and I think her Bazaar years were really her most important ones. It's always wonderful when you see a craftsman with their hands on the clothes – there was a very strong sense of seeing how to understand a woman and how to evolve a magazine."

Full Gallop by Diana Vreeland Parfums is out in November. £180 for 100ml, exclusive to Harrods
